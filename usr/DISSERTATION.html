CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background of the Study

Mathematics is a foundational subject, and fractions form a critical component of the middle school curriculum. However, students frequently encounter difficulties in understanding the concept of fractions, performing operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division) on them, and applying them to real-life situations.

1.2 Statement of the Problem

"A Study of Difficulties and Errors in Learning Fractions in Mathematics among Class VIII Students of Gajanan School with Remedial Measures."

1.3 Objectives of the Study

  1. To identify the specific difficulties and errors made by Class VIII students of Gajanan School in learning fractions.
  2. To find out the root causes of these errors.
  3. To design and implement remedial teaching strategies to overcome these difficulties.
  4. To evaluate the effectiveness of the remedial measures.

1.4 Need and Importance of the Study

Fractions are essential for higher mathematics (algebra, geometry) and daily life. Identifying errors at the Class VIII level helps in addressing misconceptions before students move to secondary education. This action research will directly benefit the students of Gajanan School by improving their fundamental mathematical skills.

CHAPTER 2: REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

A review of various educational journals and previous action research projects indicates that students commonly confuse the numerator and denominator, struggle with finding the Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) for unlike fractions, and often add or subtract denominators directly. Constructivist teaching methods and the use of concrete manipulatives (like fraction circles or strips) have been highly recommended by researchers to correct these misconceptions.

CHAPTER 3: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

3.1 Method of Research

The Action Research method was adopted for this study. Action research is best suited here because it focuses on identifying a specific, immediate problem in a classroom setting and implementing a practical intervention to solve it.

3.2 Sample

A purposive sample of 30 students from Class VIII of Gajanan School who consistently showed poor performance in mathematics unit tests was selected for the study.

3.3 Tools for Data Collection

  1. Pre-Test: A diagnostic test containing 20 questions on fractions.
  2. Post-Test: A similar test administered after remedial teaching.
  3. Observation: Noting student reactions and engagement during remedial classes.

3.4 Procedure of the Study

  1. Administration of Pre-Test: To identify the baseline performance and specific error patterns.
  2. Analysis of Errors: Categorizing the mistakes made by students.
  3. Remedial Teaching: Over 10 sessions using visual aids, fraction kits, and practical examples to re-teach the concepts.
  4. Administration of Post-Test: To measure the impact of the remedial teaching.

CHAPTER 4: DATA ANALYSIS AND INTERPRETATION

4.1 Analysis of Errors (Pre-Test)

Upon analyzing the pre-test answer sheets of the 30 students, the following major errors were identified:

Type of Error No. of Students Percentage
Adding numerators and denominators directly (e.g., 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5) 18 60%
Inability to find LCM for unlike fractions 22 73.3%
Confusion in multiplying vs. dividing fractions 15 50%
Failure to simplify the final answer to lowest terms 25 83.3%

4.2 Comparison of Pre-Test and Post-Test Scores

After the 10-day remedial teaching program, a post-test was conducted. The scores (out of 20) are summarized below.

Score Range Pre-Test (No. of Students) Post-Test (No. of Students)
0 - 5 (Very Poor) 12 0
6 - 10 (Poor) 14 4
11 - 15 (Average) 4 16
16 - 20 (Good) 0 10

Interpretation:
The table clearly shows a significant shift in student performance. Before remedial teaching, 26 out of 30 students scored below 10. After the remedial intervention, 26 out of 30 students scored above 10, with 10 students achieving the highest bracket (16-20). The average class score improved drastically.

CHAPTER 5: FINDINGS, CONCLUSIONS, AND SUGGESTIONS

5.1 Findings

  1. The most common error was adding or subtracting unlike denominators directly without finding the LCM.
  2. Students lacked a conceptual visual understanding of what a fraction represents.
  3. Remedial teaching using concrete objects (fraction kits) significantly improved understanding.
  4. The post-test results indicated a high success rate for the action research intervention.

5.2 Conclusion

The action research confirms that the difficulties faced by Class VIII students of Gajanan School in learning fractions were largely due to flawed foundational concepts and procedural memorization without understanding. The remedial measures, which focused on visual and interactive learning, were highly effective in correcting these errors and improving mathematical achievement.

5.3 Suggestions

  1. For Teachers: Regular diagnostic testing should be done to catch foundational errors early. Use visual aids rather than relying solely on abstract blackboard teaching.
  2. For the School: The school should provide mathematical kits and manipulatives for middle school classes.
  3. For Parents: Encourage students to apply fraction concepts in daily life, such as during cooking or dividing objects.
